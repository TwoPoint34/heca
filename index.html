Nature-based Home Solutions (NBHS)

In recent years, there has been exciting momentum in ecological landscaping, green infrastructure, and climate adaptation. NBHS is a revenue-generating not-for-profit created to contribute to these much-needed efforts and to develop impact and efficiency standards for the work. Inspired by the well-established "home energy audit" profession, our "home ecology audit" extends science-based benefits to the entire home landscape by:

• Directly addressing well-defined, measurable environmental challenges
• Maximizing positive social and environmental impacts through reducing cost, labor, fuel, and long-term maintenance requirements
• Saving clients money and improving their quality of life
• Integrating seamlessly into clients' existing landscaping practices

What: Create Nature-based Livelihoods



Why: NBHS brings the best of nature to your own backyard while creating skilled, dignified, comfortable livelihoods that do not rely on unpredictable external funding. Our deep understanding of ecological processes, geographic information systems (GIS), and field-proven techniques radically minimize costs and carbon emissions by nearly eliminating reliance on heavy equipment and fossil fuel usage. In fact, a motivated individual can embark on a satisfying career with only a bicycle or bus fare and a few hand tools. One way or another, people who work with NBHS will become more effective land stewards wherever they live and whatever they do for a living.

What: Reduce Localized Flooding (esp. Basements)

Why: Human environments have long struggled to deal with heavy rainfall events, and the frequency and intensity of such events is increasing in Southeast Michigan1. When properly located and designed, green infrastructure such as bioswales, rain gardens, and tree pits can substantially reduce stormwater runoff and thereby reduce load on storm sewers and corresponding flood risk (esp. basements). Mindfully-wild green infrastructure can also provide a beautiful, low-maintenance, carbon-capturing addition to any yard.

1United States Environmental Protection Agency, “Extreme Precipitation,” EPA.gov, last modified August 25, 2025, https://www.epa.gov/climatechange-science/extreme-precipitation

What: Mitigate Extreme Heat

Why: As extreme summer heat events increase2, so do the health risks of heat exposure, especially during power outages and among more vulnerable populations such as the low-income elderly. Precisely-located trees and shrubs can help reduce these risks and save on summer cooling bills.

What: Grow Food

Why: Growing your own food optimizes freshness, saves money, improves nutrition, fosters a sense of place, and is simply a lot of fun. Annual gardens are great, but they're also a lot of work. NBHS can plant perennials that you harvest year after year without re-planting. Choose typical fruit trees (cherries, apples, peaches, etc.) or experiment with native edibles as part of a truly local cuisine inspired by indigenous practices and a spirit of experimentation. Your plants will also likely feed wildlife, which can be viewed as a co-benefit, though the animals will typically leave you ample food after they've had their fill.

What: Support Native Biodiversity

Why: Join a growing community of people working ultra-locally to protect our imperiled natural wealth. Native plants support more biodiversity than non-native plants3 and they better support native wildlife4. Fortunately, many people are starting to consider hummingbirds, butterflies, bees, and other priceless pollinators to be more beautiful than golf course sterility. At our own home, my partner Cynthia and I are practicing the nature-based solutions that NBHS promotes. We initially wondered why passers-by stopped and stared at our yard on summer evenings, but then we realized that they were captivated by the chorus of lightning bugs that have sadly disappeared from so many yards.

What: Control Disease-spreading Pests

Why: As Michigan's climate gets hotter and wetter, we are seeing an increase in diseases and the pests (vectors) that spread them5. NBHS addresses this inherently ecological challenge with ecological solutions that reduce or isolate disease vectors (e.g. ticks, mosquitoes, mice). Without skilled management of one's own yard (i.e. more than merely mowing), many homeowners unwittingly create ideal habitats for mosquitoes and ticks. Over the decade that we've been lightly managing our yard, Cynthia and I have never seen a tick and have seen a dramatic decrease in mosquitoes.

What: Other Promising Benefits

Why: NBHS methods are ever-evolving and substantially shaped by feedback from our clients, assuming that our practices always remain based on sound ecological and economic principles. For example, we are eager to explore interventions to reduce risk from fire and winter weather. Although Michigan typically suffers lower fire risk than out west, NBHS is fully prepared to incorporate fire-resistence into our green infrastructure design if a client so desires. Another exciting area of exploration is the potential for precision GSI to reduce icy sidewalks and driveways. While GSI may be less effective in the winter, the research suggests that it can still offer many significant benefits (cite). NBHS can also help a homeowner explore opportunities to save money while reducing carbon footprint, such as through rooftop solar or geothermal heating and cooling. Geothermal (ground-source heat pump) powered from the grid by wind energy is really the only viable renewable heating strategy for Michigan. Solar energy is very productive in the summer but entirely inadequate for winter heating, especially at night.
