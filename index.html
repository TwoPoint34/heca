Reduce Localized Flooding (esp. Basements)

Human environments have long struggled to deal with heavy rainfall events, and the frequency and intensity of such events is increasing in Southeast Michigan (https://www.epa.gov/climatechange-science/extreme-precipitation). Properly located and designed green infrastructure such as bioswales, rain gardens, and tree pits can substantially reduce stormwater runoff and thereby reduce load on storm sewers and corresponding flood risk (esp. basements). When based on deep ecological understanding, green infrastructure can also provide a beautiful, low-maintenance, carbon-capturing addition to any yard.

Mitigate Extreme Heat

As extreme summer heat events increase (https://glisa.umich.edu/wp-content/uploads/2023/10/Climate-Hazards-Great_Lakes_GLISA_October_2023.pdf), so do the health risks of heat exposure, especially during power outages and among vulnerable populations such as the low-income elderly. Precisely-located trees and shrubs can help reduce these risks and save on summer cooling bills.

Grow Food

Growing your own food optimizes freshness, saves money, improves nutrition, fosters a sense of place, and is simply a lot of fun. Annual gardens are great, but they're a lot of work. HEcA can plant perennials that you can harvest year after year without re-planting. Choose typical fruit trees (cherries, apples, peaches, etc.) or experiment with native edibles as part of a truly local cuisine inspired by indigenous practices and a spirit of experimentation. Native plants support native animals, too, though they will typically leave you plenty of food after they've had their fill.

Support Native Biodiversity

Join a growing community of people working ultra-locally to protect our imperiled natural wealth. I personally find hummingbirds, butterflies, bees, and other priceless pollinators to be more beautiful than golf courses, and I don't think I'm the only one. At first, I wondered why people stopped and stared at our yard on summer evenings. Then I realized that they were captivated by the hundreds (thousands?) of lightning bugs that call our property home. At the same time, we have never seen a tick in our yard in the decade we've lived here (knock on wood). We've also had a major decrease in mosquitos as we've implemented ecological management practices on our property.

Control Disease Vectors

As Michigan's climate gets hotter and wetter, we are seeing an increase in diseases and the animals that spread them (cite). HEcA finds ecological solutions to address this ecological challenge by reducing or isolating disease vectors (e.g. ticks, mosquitoes, mice). Without skilled management of ones own yard (more than merely mowing), many homeowners unwittingly create ideal habitats for mosquitoes and ticks. Other Promising Benefits

HEcA's methods are ever-evolving and substantially shaped by feedback from our clients, assuming that our methods always remain based on sound ecological and economic principles. For example, we are eager to explore and implement interventions to reduce risk from fire and winter weather. Although Michigan typically suffers from less fire risk than out west, HEcA is fully prepared to incorporate fire-resistence into our GSI design if a client so desires. Another exciting area of exploration is the potential for precision GSI to reduce icy sidewalks and driveways. While GSI may be less effective in the winter, the research suggests that it can still offer many significant benefits (cite). HEcA can also help a homeowner explore opportunities to save money while reducing carbon footprint, such as through rooftop solar or geothermal heating and cooling. Geothermal (ground-source heat pump) powered from the grid by wind energy is really the only viable renewable heating strategy for Michigan. Solar energy is very productive in the summer but entirely inadequate for winter heating, especially at night.
