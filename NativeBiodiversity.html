Support Native Biodiversity

Native plants support native animals


Compared to green infrastructure with low plant diversity, multi-species (esp. native) installations are often more resilient against drought or water-logged conditions(cite). Plant diversity also tends to increase the effectiveness of co-benefits, such as stormwater runoff reduction, extreme heat mitigation, etc.(cite) This plant diversity also better supports native wildlife. NBHS identifies where new plantings (or simply reduced mowing) will yield the greatest benefits for humans and for nature. For example, lightning bugs (aka fireflies) depend on the presence of long grasses9. While few homeowners (or their neighbors) would tolerate a completely unkempt yard, letting the grass grow longer in a bioswale positioned using GIS could help lightning bugs and many other species while reducing labor and preserving aesthetics. NBHS promotes native biodiversity through:
• Removing invasive plant species
• Even on already forested plots, adding a shrub understory increases ecological niches (greater species diversity) under existing trees • Reduce and filter stormwater runoff and thereby improve downstream water quality and wildlife habitat • A given property can also be assessed in relation to existing wildlife corridors in order to improve connectivity between habitat fragments

Metrics of Success:
• Some homeowners may be inspired to act as "citizen scientists" and conduct plant and animal inventories on their properties
• NBHS can analyze this participatory data to yield a larger picture of impacts, such as in relation to wildlife corridors and habitat connectivity
• Photos and notes shared by homeowners will also help NBHS monitor the health of the green infrastructure installations over time

9Firefly Conservation & Research, “How You Can Help Prevent Fireflies from Disappearing,” Firefly.org, accessed April 10, 2026, https://www.firefly.org/how-you-can-help.html
