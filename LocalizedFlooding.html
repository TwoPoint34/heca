Reduce Localized Flooding

Use advanced spatial analysis to precisely locate green infrastructure for optimal runoff reduction.

GIS and remote sensing can be used to identify where rainfall is most likely to flow or pool on a property. In most cases these dynamics not visible to the naked eye. Depending on the clients' vision for their property, green infrastructure in these locations may take the form of planting shrubs or trees or simply reducing mowing frequency. This increase in perennial vegetation will slow and reduce stormwater runoff through increased infiltration, interception, evaporation, and surface roughness. Trees and shrubs can also be planted to overhang paved surfaces and thereby reduce rainfall reaching the ground and cooling those surfaces during hot weather. In the case of basement flooding, HEcA's GIS analysis can often be used to identify the root causes of flooding and propose interventions to reduce or eliminate it. HEcA installs low-maintenance GSI, especially shrubs and trees, though if a client prefers more management-heavy plants (e.g. native wildflowers) we can offer referrals to our partner companies.

Success of GSI interventions can be measured qualitatively by the homeowner or tenant experiencing less frequent and less severe basement flooding. Quantitative monitoring of success is also possible through somewhat pricey, given that the only reliable way to quantify runoff reduction benefits are through field measurement. That said, if a client wants to really “geek out” on their GSI's performance, rain gauges and water level dataloggers can estimate infiltration. Most clients will be satisfied knowing that GI has a well-researched basis for its effectiveness (https://www.epa.gov/system/files/documents/2021-11/bmp-bioretention-rain-gardens.pdf, “Rain garden hydrological performance – Responses to real rainfall events”).
